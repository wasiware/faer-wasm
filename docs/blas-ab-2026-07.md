everything was shaped?"): for every BLAS-level operation faer-wasm uses

## Reading

1. **Level 1/2 parity confirmed** (copy, gemv, ger, trsv): 17 of 20 rows
   are OVERLAP, and no faer win separates anywhere. These operations are
   memory-bound — a shaped loop and faer's path finish in a dead heat
   because both are waiting on the same bytes. The standing policy
   (don't shape what bandwidth already caps) holds.
2. **The surprise is Level 3.** The working number for R — gemm
   throughput over streaming-loop throughput, the quantity the whole
   shaping strategy is calibrated against — has been ~2 on the runner.
   On THIS machine, measured directly, the flat SIMD column-axpy gemm
   comes within 4–10% of faer's blocked gemm at every size (loop/faer
   0.90–0.96, never separated). syrk reads at loop-parity-or-better
   (1.00–1.17, never separated; the triangular-aware loop skips half
   the writes). Only trsm (0.83–0.87 at n≥512) and trmm at 1024 (0.94)
   produce separated faer wins.
3. **Policy consequence: none yet — the verdicts do not justify
   replacing anything.** faer's paths never lose separated; the loop's
   apparent edges never separate. The unchanged rows stay unchanged.
4. **Thesis consequence if the runner replicates this: R is
   machine-dependent and possibly much closer to 1 than 2.** That would
   sharpen, not weaken, the common-thread story: (a) the case for
   deleting mid-layer blocked machinery gets stronger (even less gemm
   advantage to buy back a surcharge σ); (b) faer's 5–20× matmul wins
   over Pyodide are then about scipy's OpenBLAS-generic gemm being far
   from the machine ceiling, not about blocking beating streaming;
   (c) the multishift's large-n win over our hand kernel must be
   credited to aggressive early deflation (fewer sweeps — an
   algorithmic effect), not gemm batching. Each of those is checkable.

## Runner replication — three draws (2026-07-18, runs 29631062796 / 29631536777 / 29631542527)

The reference-environment answer, and it inverts the container's L3
reading. Consensus across all three runner draws:

- **n ≤ 512, Level 3: the streaming loop BEATS faer's blocked path on
  every draw, 1.1–1.45×, mostly range-separated.** gemm itself: 1.17×/
  1.21×/1.18×/1.21× (draw 1, all separated), 1.31×/1.07×/1.15×/1.14×
  (draw 2), 1.33×/1.18×/1.17×/1.22× (draw 3) at n=64–512. syrk, trmm,
  trsm read the same way.
- **n = 1024 is machine-dependent**: draws 1 and 3 stay loop-ahead
  (gemm 1.21×/1.22× separated); draw 2 reads parity-to-faer (gemm
  1.01× OVERLAP, trmm 0.91× faer WINS) — a cache-size effect,
  consistent with the verdict-stability rule.
- Level 2: loop modestly ahead everywhere (1.05–1.25×, frequently
  separated). Level 1 copy: parity.

**Directly measured on the reference machine class, R ≤ 1 through
n = 512.** The container (previous section) was the outlier in
direction — desktop-class hardware is where faer's blocking looks
best, and even there it only reached parity.

## Standing consequences (recorded, not acted on — tuning freeze)

1. The kernels' "route the O(n³) bulk through faer's gemm" rule leaves
   ~15–20% on the table at L3 shapes ≤ 512 in the reference
   environment. A flat streaming gemm/trmm bulk path for the kernel
   layer is now a measured candidate lever — architect's call, next
   campaign material.
2. The common-thread thesis sharpens to its limit: on the target,
   blocked Level 3 is not merely a small win over streaming — in the
   shipping regime it is not a win at all. The two-speed machine is,
   on the reference class, one speed.
3. faer's 5–20× matmul wins over Pyodide are therefore entirely about
   scipy's OpenBLAS-generic build being far from the machine ceiling.
4. Unchanged rows stay unchanged for now: correctness machinery,
   size budget, and the freeze all say measure twice before rerouting.

Caveat kept open: all A/B builds are the plain simd128 profile; the
relaxed-SIMD (FMA) build could shift the balance toward faer's
microkernels and should be part of any rerouting campaign.

## Step 1 of the BLAS campaign: ceilings + the FMA race (2026-07-18, runs 29634584232/29634587324/29634591008)

Three runner draws, FMA build (`-C target-feature=+simd128,+relaxed-simd`),
three-way race per op: faer vs plain streaming loop vs fused-FMA streaming
loop. Fused numerics cross-checked against faer before timing.

**Ceilings (the roofline the campaign now scores against):**
- memory bandwidth (triad, 24 MB): 12.2–17.8 GB/s across draws
- peak f64 SIMD arithmetic: ~12.5 GFLOP/s plain build, **23.1–25.3
  GFLOP/s FMA build** (fusing doubles the machine's ceiling, as theory
  says)
- the streaming gemm runs at ~6.2–6.8 GFLOP/s = **~55% of the plain
  ceiling, 27% of the FMA ceiling** — the headroom the build phase
  chases (micro-tiling + per-op FMA)
- `copy` runs at the bandwidth ceiling already — Level 1/2 are done
  before they start

**Verdicts:**
1. **The FMA card does not rescue faer.** With fused instructions on
   both sides available, faer's blocked Level 3 still loses to the
   plain streaming loop on two of three draws at every size (gemm
   loop/faer 1.04–1.35), and splits the third only at n ≥ 512
   (0.94–0.97). The streaming verdict from the plain build stands.
2. **FMA is per-operation, not a blanket win, for our loops:**
   - trmm: fused loop best everywhere, all draws (+10–25% over plain,
     1.11–2.01× over faer)
   - trsm: fused best or tied (+1–13% over plain)
   - gemv: mildly better (+3–36%, small absolute times)
   - gemm: neutral (0.90–1.06 vs plain — at 27% of peak the loop is
     nowhere near compute-bound, so faster arithmetic buys nothing)
   - **syrk: fused is consistently WORSE (−9–26%, all three draws)** —
     the fused instruction's longer latency chain hurts the
     shrinking-column accumulation. A real, replicated negative.
3. Large-n machine-dependence persists (draw 3 leaned faer at
   1024) — the per-op crossover measurement stays in the build phase.

**Design consequence for the BLAS layer:** each operation gets its
variant chosen by measurement — fused for trmm/trsm/gemv, plain for
gemm/syrk — with the FMA build detected at compile time and both
variants kept behind one name. No blanket policy would have been
right: a single "use FMA everywhere" switch would have made syrk
slower and gemm no faster.

## Step 2: the Level-1 assumption race (2026-07-18, runs 29648971889 / 29648968138 / 29648964550)

The implementation-plan table carried three "no hand-SIMD" rows that an
honest audit exposed as asserted, never measured: `swap` ("compiler
auto-vectorizes"), `asum` ("bandwidth-bound; SIMD buys nothing"),
`iamax` ("the branch breaks streaming"). Architect ordered the race.
Harness: `bench/l1-ab.mjs` + `run_l1_ab` — plain scalar loop vs
hand-SIMD (simd128) variant, interleaved, 5 rounds, numeric agreement
cross-checked before timing, verdicts on range separation. ratio > 1
means SIMD is faster.

Three runner draws, three distinct machines (runners 1000000430 /
1000000429 / 1000000428), n = 256 / 512 / 1024:

| op | draw 1 | draw 2 | draw 3 | verdict |
| - | - | - | - | - |
| swap | 1.21× / 1.32× / 1.26× | 1.33× / 1.31× / 1.17× | 1.15× / 1.31× / 1.28× | SIMD WINS, all 9 rows |
| asum | 3.65× / 3.93× / 3.89× | 3.46× / 3.67× / 3.68× | 3.65× / 3.88× / 3.97× | SIMD WINS, all 9 rows |
| iamax | 1.48× / 1.46× / 1.52× | 1.39× / 1.58× / 1.56× | 1.47× / 1.44× / 1.52× | SIMD WINS 8, OVERLAP 1 (draw 1, n=256) |

(Container pre-check agreed in direction for asum 1.80–1.95× and iamax
1.23–1.76×; swap was a wash locally — another case of the container
reading differently from the reference class.)

**All three assumptions are refuted.**

1. **asum is the embarrassment: 3.5–4× left on the table** by the
   "bandwidth-bound" story. The plain loop's serial dependent-add chain
   is latency-bound, nowhere near bandwidth; two independent v128
   accumulators with `f64x2_abs` expose the parallelism.
2. **iamax's branch is beatable**: a branch-free `f64x2_pmax` pass over
   values plus one scalar rescan for the index wins 1.4–1.6×
   everywhere. "The branch breaks streaming" was true of the naive
   shape, not of the operation.
3. **swap wins 1.15–1.33×** — LLVM does auto-vectorize it, but not as
   well as the explicit loop. Margins straddle the 1.3× stability
   threshold, but the verdict is unanimous: 9 of 9 rows range-separated
   SIMD wins across three machines, so the direction is settled even
   where the magnitude is machine-weather.

**Consequence:** the plan table's swap/asum/iamax rows flip to SIMD
streaming loops. Every remaining "no" in the table is now either
measured (`copy` — raced to a dead heat and running at the bandwidth
ceiling) or structural (`rotg` — no arrays to stream). This is the
race-the-foundation rule's third catch this week: every one of the
architect's suspected unraced assumptions was wrong.
